    BMI is a measure of body fat based on height and weight. It is calculated by dividing weight in kilograms by height in meters squared (kg/mÂ²). This measure is used to screen for weight categories that may lead to health problems. BMI does not directly measure body fat, but it is an inexpensive and easy way to screen for potential weight-related health problems. BMI can be used to track progress towards weight loss goals.By weighing yourself regularly and calculating your BMI, you can see how your weight is changing over time.

    The BMI Scale

    The World Health Organization (WHO) has classified BMI into different categories, each with its own associated health risks.These categories are:
      Underweight: BMI less than 18.5
      Normal weight: BMI between 18.5 and 24.9
      Overweight: BMI between 25 and 29.9
      Obesity: BMI of 30 or higher
    Underweight
    A BMI below 18.5 is considered underweight. This can be a result of factors such as malnutrition, eating disorders, or certain medical conditions.
    Normal weight
    A normal BMI is between 18.5 and 24.9. People with a normal BMI are at lower risk for many chronic diseases, such as heart disease, stroke, type 2 diabetes, and certain types of cancer. It is a range of body weight that is considered healthy for adults.
    Overweight
    A BMI between 25 and 29.9 is considered overweight. This is a risk factor for chronic diseases such as type 2 diabetes, heart disease, and stroke.
    Obesity
    A BMI of 30 or higher is considered obese. This is a major risk factor for chronic diseases such as type 2 diabetes, heart disease, stroke, and certain types of cancer.

    Waist-Hip Ratio (WHR)

    Waist-hip ratio (WHR) is a measure of body fat distribution that is calculated by dividing the waist circumference by the hip circumference. WHR is a better predictor of health risk than BMI because it takes into account body fat distribution. To measure your waist circumference, you should place a tape measure around your waist, just above your hip bones. To measure your hip circumference, you should place a tape measure around your hips, at the widest point. You can then use the formula below to calculate your WHR. The WHO has classified WHR into different categories, each with its own associated health risks. These categories are:
      For men: Less than 1.0
      For women: Less than 0.85
    Individuals with a WHR that is higher than these levels are at increased risk of abdominal obesity, heart disease, and stroke. Abdominal obesity is a type of body fat that is stored around the organs in the abdomen. This type of fat is more harmful than fat that is stored in other parts of the body.

    In as much as flavour meals should not be said bye to, intake should be looked into.
    For instance, carbohydrate taking should be minimsed as they play a big role in weight gain. This is due to:

      1. Calorie Intake and Energy Balance:

    Weight gain occurs when the body consumes more calories than it burns. Carbohydrates, like other macronutrients, provide calories. Consuming more calories than the body needs for daily activities and energy expenditure leads to excess energy storage, primarily as body fat.
      2. Impact on Insulin Response:
    Carbohydrates, particularly refined carbohydrates with high glycemic index (GI), cause a rapid rise in blood sugar levels. The body responds by releasing insulin, a hormone that helps regulate blood sugar. While insulin is essential, excessive insulin secretion can lead to increased fat storage.
      3. Satiety and Appetite Regulation:
    Certain types of carbohydrates, such as fiber-rich whole grains and fruits, promote satiety, the feeling of fullness. When you feel satiated, you're less likely to overeat. However, refined carbohydrates, processed foods, and sugary drinks often lack fiber and can lead to overconsumption due to reduced satiety.
